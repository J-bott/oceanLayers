0000 ATM

The ocean cover 70% of Earth's surface. Yet 95% of it remains unknown. Dive in and explore the deepest parts of the ocean divided in it's 5 zones.

Epipelagic Zone

0 - 200 meters from sea level

The "Sunlight Zone". It's characterized by the sunlight that reaches this zone, allowing the presence of beings capable of photosynthesis. About 90% of marine life resides here, . This zone plays a crucial role in supporting oceanic biodiversity.

Plankton

Plankton refers to tiny organisms that float in ocean currents, encompassing both plant-like phytoplankton and animal-like zooplankton. Phytoplankton, such as algae, are essential as they generate oxygen, serve as the foundation of marine food chains, and help regulate global climate by absorbing carbon dioxide. Zooplankton, including small animals like krill, provide food for larger marine creatures, supporting biodiversity from small fish to whales. Plankton's role in marine ecosystems is critical for biodiversity maintenance, environmental health monitoring, and understanding climate impacts on oceans.

Light Penetration

The epipelagic zone tends to extend through a shorter distance in coastal waters where the water turbulence stops the penetration of light. It’s also relevant that different wavelengths of light have different penetration. So some colors, like red, are absent sooner than others.

Mesopelagic

200 - 1000 meters

At this point sunlight is so scarce that the process of photosynthesis is not possible, which gives this zone the name “twilight zone”. The biodiversity of this location is largely sustained by particles that fall from the epipelagic zone. This zone is adjacent to the continental slope.

Deepest scuba dive

332.35 meters

The world record was set by Ahmed Gabr in Dahab in the Red Sea on 18/19 September 2014. His descent took only 15 minutes while the ascent, to avoid the side effects of abrupt pressure change in the human body known as “decompression sickness”, lasted 13 hours 35 minutes.

Bathypelagic

1000 - 4000 meters from sea level

Also known as the "midnight zone” as no sunlight reaches this location. This zone is home for the continental rise. A steep inclination built by fallen materials that compose a structure similar to a slide.

Titan

A submarine transporting 5 passengers with the goal of visiting the Titanic wreckage (3789m) fatally imploded. The pressure outside of the submarine was so great that it’s calculated that the implosion of the submarine happened at one millisecond at a speed of 2414 km/h.

Abyssopelagic

4000 - 6000 meters from sea level

The abyss zone is the largest in area. It covers 83% of the ocean which is 60% of Earth’s surface. Its topology consists of flat plains being also the flattest landforms on Earth. This is also considered the ocean floor

Giant squid

Also known as the "Sunlight Zone," extends from the ocean surface down to 200 meters. It's characterized by Sunlight Penetration sunlight reaches this zone, allowing the presence of beings capable of photosynthesis. About 90% of marine life resides here, forming the base of aquatic ecosystems. This zone plays a crucial role in supporting oceanic biodiversity.

Hadopelagic

The Hadalpelagic (name derived from the word “Hades”) is not as commonly found as the other zones. This zone is created by activities of tectonic plates that form great depressions on the ocean floor that are known as trenches. Between all the trenches known to man, the Mariana trench, close to the Mariana islands is the deepest one.

Amphipod

Amphipods are small, shrimp-like crustaceans that thrive in the hadopelagic zone. These resilient creatures are highly adaptable, capable of withstanding extreme pressures and cold temperatures. Amphipods play a crucial role in the hadopelagic ecosystem by participating in the decomposition process, breaking down organic matter that sinks to the ocean floor. Their presence helps recycle nutrients and supports the deep-sea food web, making them integral to the health and sustainability of these remote and inhospitable marine environments.
